Planning Your Pregnancy

Start preparing ideally 3-6 months before trying to conceive

‚è∞ Why Planning Ahead Is So Important

Your baby's major organs ‚Äî heart, brain, spine, arms, and legs ‚Äî begin forming very early, between weeks 5 and 10 of pregnancy (just 3-6 weeks after conception).

By the time most women realize they are pregnant, this critical development has already started.

üí° This is why we recommend meeting your neurologist and obstetrician before trying to conceive ‚Äî so you have time to switch to a safer medication if needed, before your baby's organs begin to form.

üìû Talk to Your Doctor First

  • Meet your neurologist and obstetrician before trying to conceive
  • Review your current seizure medication
  • Discuss switching to a safer medication if needed
  • Do NOT stop your medication on your own ‚Äî this is dangerous
  • If seizure-free for 2+ years, discuss tapering with your doctor

üíö Start Folic Acid Early

  • Begin folic acid at least 3 months before conception
  • Women on anti-seizure medications need a higher dose (5 mg/day)
  • Folic acid reduces the risk of neural tube defects (brain and spine problems)
  • Your doctor will prescribe the right dose for you
  • Continue throughout pregnancy

üéØ Seizure Control

  • Aim for the best possible seizure control before pregnancy
  • Uncontrolled seizures are more dangerous than medication
  • Use the lowest effective dose of one medication (monotherapy)
  • Keep a seizure diary
  • Get enough sleep and manage stress

üèÉ‚Äç‚ôÄÔ∏è Healthy Lifestyle

  • Eat a balanced, nutritious diet
  • Avoid alcohol, smoking, and recreational drugs
  • Exercise regularly (as advised by your doctor)
  • Get 7-8 hours of sleep ‚Äî sleep deprivation triggers seizures
  • Take prenatal vitamins

üè• Planning Years Ahead? Consider Surgery First

If you have drug-resistant focal epilepsy and are planning pregnancy in the distant future (3-5 years), surgical evaluation may give you the best chance at a healthy, seizure-free pregnancy. Studies show women who achieve post-surgical seizure freedom have pregnancy outcomes comparable to the general population.

Why Consider Evaluation?

  • Up to 60-80% seizure freedom for suitable temporal lobe candidates
  • 40-60% for extra-temporal cases (varies by pathology)
  • Potential to reduce or stop medications after 12-24 months
  • Fewer seizures + fewer drugs = safer pregnancy

Realistic Timeline

  • Pre-surgical evaluation: 6-24 months (complex cases need longer)
  • Surgery + recovery: 3-6 months
  • Observation + medication reduction: 12-24 months
  • Then ‚Üí Plan pregnancy when seizure-free & stable

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Things to Know

  • Not everyone qualifies. Many patients are evaluated and found unsuitable for surgery. This is still valuable information for planning.
  • Surgery has risks. Like any brain surgery, there are risks including infection, bleeding, and potential effects on memory or language (1-3% major complication rate).
  • Not a guarantee. Seizures may recur, and some women may still need medications during pregnancy even after surgery.
  • Getting evaluated is not a commitment. You can decide not to proceed at any stage.
  • Requires a specialized center. Only comprehensive epilepsy centers with experienced teams can offer this. In India, there are 30-40 such centers.

üí° Who should consider this? Women with drug-resistant epilepsy (continuing seizures despite 2 or more appropriately chosen medications at adequate doses), focal epilepsy with an identifiable focus, and planning pregnancy in 3-5 years. Talk to an epileptologist at a comprehensive epilepsy center to see if you're a candidate.

üíä Taking More Than One Medication? (Polytherapy)

Polytherapy means taking 2 or more seizure medications at the same time. Some women need this to control their seizures ‚Äî and that is okay.

Here is what the research tells us:

  • ‚Üí Polytherapy without valproate: About 5-8 out of 100 babies may have birth defects
  • ‚Üí Polytherapy that includes valproate: Up to 15-17 out of 100 babies may have birth defects

If you are on more than one medication, talk to your neurologist about whether simplifying to one medication (monotherapy) is safe for you. This is best done before pregnancy.

üíö Reassurance: Sometimes taking two medications is the right choice ‚Äî especially if one medication alone does not control your seizures. Uncontrolled seizures during pregnancy can be more dangerous than the medications. Trust your neurologist to find the best balance for you.

During Your Pregnancy

A trimester-by-trimester guide to managing epilepsy during pregnancy

üå± First Trimester (Weeks 1-12)

The most critical period for your baby's organ development.

  • Contact your GP/neurologist as soon as you know you're pregnant
  • Continue your medication ‚Äî do not stop
  • Continue folic acid (5mg daily)
  • Your doctor may check your medication blood levels
  • Get your first ultrasound scan (dating scan) ‚Äî confirms your due date
  • Early anomaly scan (NT scan) at 11-14 weeks ‚Äî screens for chromosomal conditions and checks baby's early development
  • Report any changes in seizure frequency immediately
  • Morning sickness? Ask about timing your medication

üåø Second Trimester (Weeks 13-26)

Detailed scans and monitoring.

  • Detailed anomaly scan (Level 2 ultrasound) at 18-22 weeks ‚Äî a thorough check of your baby's brain, heart, spine, and other organs. This is especially important for women on anti-seizure medications.
  • Blood levels of your medication may drop ‚Äî dose adjustments may be needed
  • Lamotrigine clearance can increase up to 300% ‚Äî regular monitoring essential
  • Levetiracetam clearance increases 40-60%
  • Stay in touch with both your neurologist and obstetrician
  • Keep your seizure diary updated

üå∏ Third Trimester (Weeks 27-40)

Preparing for delivery.

  • Discuss your birth plan with your team
  • Ensure your hospital knows about your epilepsy
  • Have emergency seizure medication prescribed and ready
  • Vitamin K may be recommended for your baby at birth
  • Plan for breastfeeding ‚Äî most ASMs are safe
  • Discuss medication dose adjustments after delivery
  • Prepare seizure first aid instructions for your family

üö® When to Go to the Hospital Immediately

Seek emergency care if you have: a tonic-clonic (convulsive) seizure lasting more than 5 minutes, repeated seizures without recovery, a seizure with a fall or injury, vaginal bleeding after a seizure, or if you feel your baby is not moving normally after a seizure.

ü©∫ Having seizures during pregnancy? If your seizure control has changed since becoming pregnant, don't adjust medications on your own. Contact your neurologist ‚Äî most situations can be managed safely with proper guidance.

After Your Baby Arrives

Caring for yourself and your baby after delivery

ü§± Breastfeeding

Most anti-seizure medications are safe during breastfeeding. Levetiracetam, lamotrigine, and valproate pass into breast milk in small amounts and are generally considered compatible with breastfeeding.

The benefits of breastfeeding usually outweigh the small medication exposure. Your baby was already exposed to your medication during pregnancy ‚Äî breast milk levels are typically much lower.

Discuss with your doctor, but do not avoid breastfeeding just because you take seizure medication.

üíä Medication After Delivery

If your dose was increased during pregnancy, it may need to be reduced within days to weeks after delivery to avoid side effects.

This is especially important for lamotrigine ‚Äî levels can rise rapidly after delivery, causing dizziness or double vision.

Your doctor will guide the dose adjustment. Do not change doses on your own.

üò¥ Sleep & Seizure Risk

Sleep deprivation is the #1 seizure trigger in the postpartum period. New parents lose a lot of sleep ‚Äî this is real and important.

Tips: Share night feeds with a partner or family member. Sleep when the baby sleeps. Accept help. If breastfeeding, pump so others can do some feeds.

Keep your medication schedule consistent even when exhausted.

üõ°Ô∏è Baby Safety Precautions

If you have seizures, take simple precautions:

‚Ä¢ Change and feed your baby on the floor, not on a bed or changing table
‚Ä¢ Bathe your baby with another adult present
‚Ä¢ Use a pram with brakes when walking
‚Ä¢ Avoid carrying your baby near stairs if seizures are not controlled
‚Ä¢ Make sure family members know seizure first aid

üíú Your Mental Health Matters

Women with epilepsy are 2-3 times more likely to experience depression and anxiety during and after pregnancy. This is a medical condition, not a weakness ‚Äî and it is not your fault.

Watch for these signs:

‚Ä¢ Feeling sad or empty most of the day, for more than 2 weeks
‚Ä¢ Losing interest in things you used to enjoy
‚Ä¢ Difficulty bonding with or feeling connected to your baby
‚Ä¢ Feeling very anxious, fearful, or overwhelmed
‚Ä¢ Thoughts of harming yourself ‚Äî please tell someone immediately

Some seizure medications can also affect mood. Levetiracetam (Keppra/Levera) can cause irritability or mood changes. Lamotrigine (Lamictal/Lamez) can actually help stabilise mood. If you notice mood changes, tell your neurologist ‚Äî a medication adjustment may help.

Your doctor can screen you using a simple questionnaire called the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale ‚Äî it takes just a few minutes. Ask about it at your postpartum visits.

üôè Seeking help is not weakness ‚Äî it is strength. Depression is a medical condition just like epilepsy. It can be treated. Talk to your doctor, talk to your family, and know that you deserve support.

Contraception & Family Planning

Not all birth control methods work the same when you take seizure medication. Plan ahead with your doctors.

üí° Why This Matters

An unplanned pregnancy means you may not have time to switch to a safer medication or start folic acid early. Planning your pregnancy is the single best thing you can do for your baby's health. Good contraception gives you that control ‚Äî but you need to choose the right method.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Two-Way Problem

  • Some seizure medications reduce the effect of birth control pills. These include carbamazepine (Tegretol/Zen Retard), phenytoin (Eptoin), phenobarbital (Gardenal), and topiramate (Topamac). They make your body break down the pill faster, so it may not work.
  • It also works the other way: combined birth control pills (with estrogen) can reduce lamotrigine (Lamictal/Lamez) levels by up to 50%. This means more breakthrough seizures when you START the pill, and possible side effects when you STOP it.
  • If you take lamotrigine and want to use birth control pills, your neurologist may need to adjust your dose ‚Äî and adjust it again when you stop the pill.

‚úÖ Best Options

  • Copper IUD (Copper-T / Multiload): No hormones at all. Works for 5-10 years. NOT affected by any seizure medication. One of the best choices.
  • Hormonal IUD (Mirena / LNG-IUS): Releases a tiny amount of hormone locally. NOT affected by seizure medications. Works for 5 years. Very effective.
  • Depo-Provera injection (DMPA): Given every 3 months. Generally safe with seizure medications. Some guidelines suggest a shortened interval of 10 weeks instead of 12 weeks with enzyme-inducing medications ‚Äî ask your doctor.
  • Condoms: Always safe, no drug interactions. Can be used alongside other methods for extra protection.

üÜò Emergency Contraception

  • If you take an enzyme-inducing medication (carbamazepine, phenytoin, phenobarbital, topiramate), the standard emergency pill (i-pill / Unwanted 72) may not work as well.
  • You may need a double dose (3 mg levonorgestrel instead of 1.5 mg) ‚Äî ask your doctor or pharmacist.
  • A copper IUD inserted within 5 days is the most effective emergency contraception ‚Äî and it is NOT affected by your medication.
  • Bottom line: Talk to your neurologist AND gynaecologist about contraception. Not all methods work the same with seizure medications.

üíú Remember: Choosing the right contraception is an important part of epilepsy care. Your neurologist and gynaecologist can work together to find the best method for you.

For Families: How to Support Her

A message for husbands, parents, and in-laws

üö´ Common Myths ‚Äî Let's Clear Them

‚ùå

"Epilepsy is a curse or punishment"

Epilepsy is a medical condition of the brain ‚Äî like diabetes or asthma. It is NOT a curse, NOT contagious, and NOT anyone's fault.

‚ùå

"She should stop her medication because she is pregnant"

NEVER stop her medication. Stopping suddenly can cause dangerous seizures that harm both mother and baby. Her doctor will manage her medicine safely.

‚ùå

"The baby will get seizures from breastfeeding"

This is not true. Most seizure medications are safe during breastfeeding. The amount in breast milk is very small. Breastfeeding is good for the baby.

‚úÖ

"She CAN have a normal delivery"

Most women with epilepsy have normal vaginal deliveries. Epilepsy alone is not a reason for a caesarean section.

ü§ù How You Can Help

üò¥ Help Her Sleep

  • Sleep deprivation is the #1 seizure trigger. This is the most important thing you can do.
  • Share night feeds ‚Äî use expressed breast milk or formula for some feeds
  • Let her rest during the day when the baby sleeps
  • Take turns with the baby so she gets at least 6-7 hours of sleep

üè• Be Involved in Her Care

  • Go with her to doctor appointments ‚Äî your support matters
  • Help her remember her medication ‚Äî set reminders
  • Keep a seizure diary together
  • Understand her medication ‚Äî know the name, dose, and timing

üÜò Learn Seizure First Aid

  • Stay calm. Most seizures stop on their own in 1-3 minutes.
  • Gently turn her on her side (recovery position)
  • Clear the area of sharp or hard objects
  • Do NOT put anything in her mouth ‚Äî no spoons, no keys, no fingers
  • Do NOT try to hold her down or restrain her
  • If the seizure lasts more than 5 minutes, call an ambulance (108/112)
  • Make sure the baby is in a safe place (crib or playpen) first

üôè She is not weak. She is not a burden. She is managing a medical condition while bringing new life into the world. Your love and support make all the difference.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions from women with epilepsy who are pregnant or planning pregnancy

Can I have a normal delivery, or do I need a C-section? ‚ñº
Most women with epilepsy can have a normal vaginal delivery. Epilepsy alone is not a reason for a C-section. However, if you have frequent seizures near your due date, your doctor may recommend planned delivery or C-section. Discuss your options with your obstetrician.
Will my baby have epilepsy too? ‚ñº
The risk is low. If one parent has epilepsy, the chance of the child developing epilepsy is about 4-5% (compared to 1-2% in the general population). Most children born to mothers with epilepsy do NOT develop epilepsy. Genetic counseling can help if you have concerns about inherited forms of epilepsy.
I found out I'm pregnant and I'm on Valproate. What should I do? ‚ñº
Do not stop your medication suddenly. Contact your neurologist urgently (within 24-48 hours). Your doctor will assess whether a medication switch is possible and safe at your stage of pregnancy. Stopping valproate abruptly can cause dangerous seizures. Your doctor will guide you through this carefully.
Why do I need more folic acid than other pregnant women? ‚ñº
Some anti-seizure medications (especially valproate, carbamazepine, phenytoin) reduce folic acid levels in your body. Low folic acid increases the risk of neural tube defects (problems with the baby's brain and spine). Women on ASMs are typically prescribed 5 mg/day of folic acid ‚Äî much higher than the standard 0.4 mg. Start at least 3 months before trying to conceive.
Will my seizures get worse during pregnancy? ‚ñº
For most women, seizure frequency stays the same or even improves. About 1 in 3 women may experience more seizures, usually due to: medication blood levels dropping (your blood volume increases during pregnancy), sleep deprivation, nausea leading to missed doses, or stress. Regular blood level monitoring and dose adjustments can help prevent breakthrough seizures.
Can I take my medication during labor? ‚ñº
Yes, and you should. Take your regular medication even during labor. If you cannot take oral medication, your hospital team can give intravenous alternatives. Make sure your birth plan clearly states your epilepsy medications and that the delivery team is aware.
Is it safe to get an epidural? ‚ñº
Yes. Epidural anesthesia is generally safe for women with epilepsy and can actually be beneficial ‚Äî it reduces pain and stress, which may help prevent seizures during labor. Inform the anesthesiologist about your epilepsy and medications.
What if I have a seizure while I'm alone with my baby? ‚ñº
If your seizures are not fully controlled, try to avoid being alone with your baby when possible. Keep your baby in a safe place (crib, playpen) when you're alone. Feed and change on the floor. Have a plan in place ‚Äî teach family members and helpers seizure first aid. Consider wearing a medical alert bracelet. Many women with well-controlled epilepsy care for their babies safely and independently.
Do I need to see a special doctor? ‚ñº
Ideally, your care should involve a team: a neurologist or epileptologist (for seizure management), a high-risk obstetrician (for pregnancy care), and your GP/family doctor. Many hospitals in India have high-risk pregnancy clinics that coordinate this care. Ask your neurologist for a referral if needed.
Can I take newer medications like Lacosamide or Brivaracetam? ‚ñº
There is limited data on newer ASMs during pregnancy. Lacosamide, brivaracetam, cenobamate, and perampanel do not yet have enough pregnancy registry data to confirm their safety. If you're on one of these medications, discuss with your neurologist ‚Äî they may recommend switching to a better-studied option like lamotrigine or levetiracetam before conception.
